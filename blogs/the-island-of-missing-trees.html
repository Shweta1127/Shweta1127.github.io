Summary

The Island of Missing Trees, written by Elif Shafak, is a tender and quietly hopeful story about love, loss, and the way memories take root across generations. At its heart are Kostas and Defne, a Greek Cypriot and a Turkish Cypriot who fall in love on a divided island, where even affection can feel like an act of defiance.

Their meetings take place in the gentle refuge of a tavern, shaded by a fig tree that becomes more than just a witness—it feels like a keeper of secrets, holding their laughter, fears, and fragile hopes. But as conflict deepens in Cyprus, their world fractures, and they are forced apart by forces much larger than themselves.

The Fig Tree Speaks

Years later, the story shifts to London, where their daughter Ada grows up carrying emotions she cannot fully name. There is a quiet heaviness in her, shaped by a past she only partly understands. What's unusual and strangely beautiful is that the fig tree itself becomes a storyteller, offering a perspective that feels both grounded and poetic, reminding us how nature absorbs human histories in ways we often overlook.

Despite the sorrow threaded through the novel, there's a soft sense of resilience. Love, even when interrupted, doesn't entirely disappear—it changes form, lingers, and sometimes finds its way back through unexpected paths.

"Healing is not about forgetting but about learning how to carry the past with a little more gentleness. Like the fig tree that survives being uprooted and replanted, people too can endure, adapt, and quietly begin again." — The Island of Missing Trees, Elif Shafak

What Lingers

The characters are imperfect and wounded, yet they keep reaching for connection, even when it feels risky. The story is a powerful reminder of the human capacity for love, hope, and resilience in the face of adversity.

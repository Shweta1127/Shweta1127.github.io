Frankenstein

by Mary Shelley

✍ Shweta Suryavanshi 📅 June 12, 2026 ⏱ 8 min read
Author Mary Shelley
Language English
Published 1818
Genre Gothic Fiction
Setting Europe
Themes Creation, Responsibility, Isolation

Summary

Mary Shelley's 'Frankenstein' tells the story of Victor Frankenstein, a young scientist who creates a monstrous being from dead body parts, and his subsequent struggle with the consequences of his actions. The novel explores the complexities of human nature through the characters of Victor and his creation, who grapples with his own identity and purpose. As the story unfolds, the lines between creator and creation become increasingly blurred, leading to a tragic confrontation. The novel raises fundamental questions about the nature of humanity and what it means to be alive.

At the heart of the novel is the central conflict between Victor's ambition and his responsibility towards his creation. As the creature demands recognition and acceptance, Victor is forced to confront the horror of his own making, leading to a downward spiral of guilt, shame, and ultimately, devastating loss. The story arc is a masterful exploration of the dangers of unchecked ambition and the devastating consequences of playing God. Through the characters' struggles, Shelley raises important questions about the ethics of scientific discovery and the limits of human knowledge.

The Monstrous 'Other'

One of the most compelling themes in 'Frankenstein' is the concept of the 'other', embodied by the creature who is rejected by society due to his appearance. The novel highlights the dangers of prejudice and the consequences of fear and misunderstanding, as the creature is forced to live on the margins of society, seeking acceptance and companionship. Through the creature's story, Shelley sheds light on the complexities of human relationships and the need for empathy and understanding.

The creature's narrative also serves as a powerful commentary on the social and cultural norms of the time, highlighting the tensions between nature and nurture, and the consequences of societal rejection. As the creature struggles to find his place in the world, he is forced to confront the harsh realities of a society that fears and rejects him, leading to a deep sense of isolation and loneliness. Shelley's exploration of these themes remains hauntingly relevant today, serving as a powerful reminder of the importance of compassion and acceptance.

"It was on a dreary night of November that I beheld the accomplishment of my toils" — Frankenstein, Mary Shelley

What Lingers

Reading 'Frankenstein' is a deeply unsettling experience, one that lingers long after the final page is turned. The novel's exploration of the human condition, with all its complexities and flaws, is both haunting and thought-provoking, leaving the reader to ponder the consequences of our actions and the responsibilities that come with knowledge and power. As I reflected on the novel, I found myself drawn back to the creature's narrative, haunted by his story and the tragic events that unfold.

What stays with me long after finishing the book is the sense of empathy and understanding that Shelley so masterfully evokes. The novel's ability to evoke feelings of compassion and sorrow for the creature, despite his monstrous appearance, is a testament to the power of storytelling and the importance of human connection. 'Frankenstein' is a novel that will stay with me for a long time, a reminder of the dangers of fear and prejudice, and the importance of embracing our shared humanity.

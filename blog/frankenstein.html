Frankenstein

by Mary Shelley

✍ Shweta Suryavanshi 📅 June 16, 2026 ⏱ 10 min read
Author Mary Shelley
Language English
Published 1818
Genre Gothic Fiction
Setting Europe
Themes Creation, Responsibility, Isolation

Summary

Mary Shelley's Frankenstein tells the story of Victor Frankenstein, a young scientist who creates a monstrous creature from dead body parts. The creature, often mistakenly called Frankenstein, is shunned by its creator and left to navigate the world on its own. As the creature seeks revenge against its creator, it kills several people close to Victor, leading to a downward spiral of destruction and tragedy. The novel explores the complexities of human nature and the consequences of playing God.

The central conflict of the novel revolves around the creature's desire for acceptance and companionship, which is consistently denied by its creator and society. As the story unfolds, Victor's obsession with his creation leads to a series of tragic events, including the death of his loved ones. The creature, in turn, becomes increasingly vengeful and violent, leading to a final confrontation between the two. Through this narrative, Shelley raises important questions about the nature of monstrosity and what it means to be human.

The Monster Within

One of the most striking aspects of Frankenstein is its exploration of the monster within each of us. The creature, often seen as a symbol of the 'other,' serves as a mirror to humanity's darker impulses and desires. Through the creature's narrative, Shelley sheds light on the complexities of human psychology, revealing the capacity for both good and evil that exists within us all. This theme is particularly relevant in today's society, where we are constantly confronted with our own darker impulses and the consequences of our actions.

The creature's narrative also raises important questions about the nature of identity and what it means to be human. As the creature navigates the world, it grapples with its own sense of self, seeking to understand its place in the world and its relationship to its creator. This struggle serves as a powerful commentary on the human condition, highlighting the fragility and complexity of human identity. Through the creature's story, Shelley reminds us that our sense of self is always in flux, shaped by our experiences, relationships, and interactions with the world around us.

"It was on a dreary night of November that I beheld the accomplishment of my toils." — Frankenstein, Mary Shelley

What Lingers

Reading Frankenstein has had a profound impact on me, leaving me with a deep sense of sadness and introspection. The novel's exploration of the human condition, with all its complexities and flaws, has forced me to confront my own darker impulses and desires. The creature's narrative, in particular, has stayed with me, serving as a powerful reminder of the importance of empathy and compassion in our interactions with others.

Frankenstein will stay with me for a long time, not just because of its thought-provoking themes and memorable characters, but also because of its ability to evoke strong emotions and reflections. The novel's exploration of the human condition, with all its complexities and flaws, has left an indelible mark on my psyche, reminding me of the importance of self-awareness, empathy, and compassion. As I reflect on the novel's impact, I am reminded of the power of literature to shape our understanding of ourselves and the world around us.

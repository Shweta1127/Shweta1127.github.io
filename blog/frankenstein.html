Frankenstein

by Mary Shelley

✍ Shweta Suryavanshi 📅 June 15, 2026 ⏱ 10 min read
Author Mary Shelley
Language English
Published 1818
Genre Gothic Fiction
Setting Europe
Themes Creation, Responsibility, Isolation

Summary

Mary Shelley's Frankenstein tells the story of Victor Frankenstein, a young scientist who creates a monstrous creature from dead body parts, bringing it to life through an unnatural spark. The creature, often mistakenly called Frankenstein, is shunned by its creator and left to navigate the world alone. As the creature seeks revenge against its creator, it commits a series of tragic events that ultimately lead to devastating consequences. The novel explores the complexities of human nature and the dangers of unchecked ambition.

The central conflict of the novel revolves around the creature's desire for companionship and acceptance, which is constantly thwarted by its grotesque appearance and the fear it inspires in others. As the story unfolds, Victor Frankenstein is forced to confront the consequences of his actions, and the creature's actions become increasingly violent and destructive. The novel's story arc is a tragic exploration of the consequences of playing God and the devastating effects of loneliness and rejection. Through the characters' struggles, Shelley raises important questions about the nature of monstrosity and what it means to be human.

The Monster Within

One of the most compelling themes in Frankenstein is the idea that the monster is not just a physical creature, but also a symbol of the darker aspects of human nature. The creature's actions are a manifestation of the fears, desires, and impulses that lie beneath the surface of human consciousness. As the novel progresses, it becomes clear that the creature is not just a mindless beast, but a complex and multifaceted being with its own thoughts, feelings, and motivations.

The creature's story serves as a powerful commentary on the social and cultural norms of Shelley's time, highlighting the ways in which society can create and perpetuate monstrosity. Through the creature's experiences, Shelley critiques the notion that certain individuals or groups are inherently inferior or less deserving of respect and dignity. The novel suggests that true monstrosity lies not in physical appearance, but in the ways in which we treat others and the harm we inflict upon them.

"I had desired it with an ardour that far exceeded moderation; but now that I had finished, the beauty of the dream vanished, and breathless horror and disgust filled my heart." — Frankenstein, Mary Shelley

What Lingers

Reading Frankenstein was a deeply unsettling experience that left me feeling haunted and disturbed. The novel's exploration of the darker aspects of human nature resonated deeply with me, and I found myself reflecting on my own fears, desires, and impulses. The creature's story is a powerful reminder of the importance of empathy, compassion, and understanding, and the devastating consequences that can result when we fail to extend these qualities to others.

Frankenstein will stay with me for a long time, not just because of its masterful storytelling and vivid characters, but also because of its profound insights into the human condition. The novel's themes and motifs continue to resonate with contemporary issues and concerns, making it a work of enduring relevance and importance. As I closed the book, I felt a sense of awe and reverence for Shelley's genius, and a deep appreciation for the ways in which her novel continues to challenge and inspire readers to this day.

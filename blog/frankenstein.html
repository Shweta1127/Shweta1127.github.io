Frankenstein

by Mary Shelley

✍ Shweta Suryavanshi 📅 June 15, 2026 ⏱ 10 min read
Author Mary Shelley
Language English
Published 1818
Genre Gothic Fiction
Setting Europe
Themes Creation, Responsibility, Isolation

Summary

The novel tells the story of Victor Frankenstein, a young scientist who creates a monstrous creature from dead body parts and brings it to life. The creature, often mistakenly called Frankenstein, is rejected by its creator and left to navigate the world on its own. As the creature seeks acceptance and companionship, it becomes increasingly violent and vengeful towards its creator. The novel explores the complexities of human nature and the dangers of unchecked ambition.

The central conflict of the novel revolves around the creature's desire for human connection and its creator's refusal to accept responsibility for his actions. As the story unfolds, the creature seeks revenge against Frankenstein, leading to a series of tragic events that ultimately destroy both the creature and its creator. Through this narrative, Shelley explores the consequences of playing God and the importance of considering the ethical implications of scientific discovery. The novel's story arc is a cautionary tale about the dangers of unchecked power and the importance of human empathy.

The Monster Within

One of the most compelling aspects of Frankenstein is the way it explores the nature of monstrosity and what it means to be human. The creature, often depicted as a mindless beast, is actually a complex and multifaceted character with its own thoughts, feelings, and desires. Through the creature's narrative, Shelley raises important questions about the boundaries between human and non-human, and challenges readers to consider the ways in which we define and treat those who are different from ourselves.

The creature's story also serves as a powerful commentary on the social and cultural norms of Shelley's time. The novel highlights the ways in which societal expectations and norms can lead to the marginalization and exclusion of certain groups, and argues for a more inclusive and compassionate understanding of human nature. By exploring the complexities of the creature's character, Shelley encourages readers to think critically about the ways in which we construct and reinforce social norms, and to consider the consequences of our actions on those who are most vulnerable.

"It was on a dreary night of November that I beheld the accomplishment of my toils." — Frankenstein, Mary Shelley

What Lingers

Reading Frankenstein was a deeply unsettling experience that left me feeling both disturbed and moved. The novel's exploration of the human condition, with all its complexities and flaws, resonated deeply with me and challenged me to think critically about my own values and assumptions. The creature's story, in particular, struck a chord, and I found myself feeling a deep sense of empathy and compassion for this misunderstood and mistreated being.

Frankenstein is a novel that will stay with me for a long time, haunting my thoughts and challenging my assumptions about the world. Its exploration of the human condition, with all its complexities and flaws, is both deeply unsettling and profoundly moving. The novel's themes of creation, responsibility, and isolation are timeless and universal, and its commentary on the social and cultural norms of our time is both timely and thought-provoking. I highly recommend Frankenstein to anyone who loves literature, science, or philosophy, and is looking for a novel that will challenge and inspire them.

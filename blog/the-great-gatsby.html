The Great Gatsby

by F. Scott Fitzgerald

✍ Shweta Suryavanshi 📅 June 16, 2026 ⏱ 8 min read
Author F. Scott Fitzgerald
Language English
Published 1925
Genre Classic Fiction
Setting 1920s New York, USA
Themes Wealth, Love, Illusion, American Dream

Summary

The Great Gatsby, written by F. Scott Fitzgerald, is a classic novel set in the 1920s that revolves around the mysterious millionaire Jay Gatsby and his obsession with winning back his lost love, Daisy Buchanan. The story is narrated by Nick Carraway, a young man who moves to Long Island and becomes friends with Gatsby. As the story unfolds, the complexities of Gatsby's character and the corrupting influence of wealth are revealed. The novel is a poignant portrayal of the American Dream and its illusions.

The central conflict of the novel arises from Gatsby's doomed pursuit of Daisy, who is married to the wealthy and brutish Tom Buchanan. As Gatsby and Daisy's affair is rekindled, the tension between Gatsby and Tom builds, ultimately leading to a tragic confrontation. Through the eyes of Nick Carraway, the reader is drawn into the world of excess and superficiality, where the old money and the new rich collide. The novel's story arc is a commentary on the emptiness and moral decay of the wealthy elite.

The Elusive American Dream

The concept of the American Dream is a pervasive theme in The Great Gatsby, symbolizing the idea of self-made success and the pursuit of happiness. Gatsby's rags-to-riches story is a quintessential example of the American Dream, but it is also a commentary on the illusion of social mobility. The novel highlights the tension between the old money and the new rich, revealing the class divisions and social stratification that underlie the American society.

The green light across the water, which Gatsby longingly gazes at, is a powerful symbol of the elusive American Dream. It represents the unattainable goal that Gatsby strives for, a goal that is always just out of reach. The green light is also a symbol of the class divide, separating the old money from the new rich, and highlighting the impossibility of truly crossing over from one world to the other.

"So we beat on, boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past" — The Great Gatsby, F. Scott Fitzgerald

What Lingers

Reading The Great Gatsby is a deeply emotional experience, as it transports the reader to a world of excess and superficiality, where the characters are trapped in their own private hells. The novel's poignant portrayal of the human condition, with all its flaws and vulnerabilities, is a powerful reminder of the fragility of human relationships and the corrupting influence of wealth and power.

The Great Gatsby is a novel that will stay with you long after you finish reading it, its themes and characters lingering in your mind like a haunting melody. The novel's exploration of the human condition, with all its complexities and contradictions, is a powerful reminder of the importance of empathy and understanding in our own lives. The Great Gatsby is a timeless classic that will continue to captivate readers for generations to come.
